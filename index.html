MA school districts still aren't reporting how often students are arrested

A 2018 state law required Massachusetts to start collecting and publishing data on school-based arrests of students. I found that five years later, only one in 10 districts and charter schools are doing so. Advocates say the data is sorely needed to assess the impact of policing in schools.

By MARINA VILLENEUVE

Published August 10, 2023, 9 a.m. EST

The 2018 Criminal Justice Reform Act required superintendents and police chiefs in Massachusetts to enter into written agreements to specify who's responsible for collecting and reporting data on school-based arrests, citations and court referrals to the state

Data collection started in the 2018-2019 school year, and the first year of reporting was spotty.

But five years later, concerns raised by advocate group Citizens for Juvenile Justice years ago are still lingering. The latest state data for the 2021-2022 school year shows that well over 300 Massachusetts school districts and charter schools still aren't reporting any school arrests.




Massachusetts defines "school-based arrests" as arrests for activity conducted on school grounds, during off-campus school activities or due to a referral from a school official.




Juvenile justice advocates say the lack of transparency makes it impossible to understand the impact of police in schools.

Boston Public Schools, which educates over 54,000 students at 125 schools, reported 10 arrests.

Boston Public Schools halted the use of police officers in schools by 2021, which could account for that number.

Still, there's evidence that number is an undercount: in the 2018-2019 school year, the school district reported four school-based arrests.

But the school district told WBUR there were actually 114 arrests that school year.




Meanwhile, districts statewide are still facing challenges including violence on school grounds, on top of calls for greater emphasis on hiring more mental health professionals equipped to handled crises.

The sparse available data on arrests shows the vast majority of students are classified as having "high needs." That means they may be economically disadvantaged, a current or former English Language Learner or a student with disabilities.





The reported data shows students are being arrested for offenses including physical attacks, weapons on school grounds, threats of attacks and illegal substances.



Massachusetts doesn't require schools to report what happens to students following arrests.




I've reached out to several school districts to get a sense of the discrepancy between what they report to the state and the actual data.

So far, a spokesperson for Lowell Public Schools said the district had 6 school-based arrests in the 2020-2021 school year.

That includes arrests of 3 students for possession of BB guns, two assault arrests and another arrest for assault on a police officer.

In contrast, the district reported just one arrest to the state for physical battery.
